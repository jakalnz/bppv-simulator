Technical White Paper · Vestibular Simulation

Modeling BPPV: physics, assumptions, and open questions in an interactive canal-and-cupula simulator

A working account of what the simulator computes, why each constant and simplification was chosen, where the model's own comments flag it as provisional, and what would most improve it next.

Subject bppv-simulator (Three.js / TypeScript) Scope physics layer + maneuver scripts + rendering Status living document, generated from source
Abstract

This simulator renders posterior- and horizontal-canal benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV) — both canalithiasis and cupulolithiasis variants — as an interactive 3D scene driven by a small chain of coupled physical models: canal geometry, an overdamped clot-transport model, a first-order cupula relaxation, and a vestibulo-ocular reflex (VOR) that produces torsional/vertical/horizontal nystagmus. Nearly every constant in the codebase carries an inline citation or a documented rationale, which this paper collects and organizes. The intent is diagnostic teaching, not clinical measurement: waypoint angles, hold durations, and settling speeds are compressed or approximated for legibility, and each such choice is flagged where it appears in the source. Several genuine open questions remain — a ~16° vs ~30° discrepancy in horizontal-canal tilt, an unverified geotropic/apogeotropic sign convention, and a short-arm re-entry pathway that is implemented but currently disabled pending tuning.

1What the simulator does

A single-page app that lets a user place debris in one of two canals under one of two pathology mechanisms, drive the head through a diagnostic or repositioning maneuver, and watch the resulting eye movement play out in real time.

Canals & pathology selectable

Posterior and horizontal semicircular canals are modeled (anterior-canal anatomy exists in the underlying dataset but is not wired into the simulator). Each canal can be assigned canalithiasis (free-floating otoconial debris inside the duct) or cupulolithiasis (debris adherent directly to the cupula), the two recognized BPPV mechanisms. Cupulolithiasis carries a further debrisOnUtricularSide toggle distinguishing the geotropic and apogeotropic variants, most clinically relevant for the horizontal canal.

Maneuvers scripted playback

  • Dix-Hallpike — posterior-canal diagnostic positioning.
  • Epley — posterior-canal canalith repositioning procedure.
  • Semont diagnostic & liberatory — posterior-canal rapid-reposition maneuvers.
  • Supine head-roll test (Pagnini–McClure) — horizontal-canal diagnostic.
  • BBQ roll (Lempert) — horizontal-canal repositioning.
  • Zuma e Maia maneuver — for apogeotropic horizontal-canal cupulolithiasis.

Every maneuver is written as a single side-parametrized function rather than hand-mirrored left/right copies, and played back through a shared ManeuverPlayer that slerps between waypoint quaternions.

Interaction modes

Head orientation can come from scripted maneuver playback, free mouse-drag, or a device gyroscope — the same physics pipeline consumes all three via a common orientation-source interface.

Visualization

A 3D canal cutaway (idealized "basic" duct, a "realistic" real-anatomy glass overlay traced from cadaveric data, and a zoomed "detailed" schematic view of the ampulla/cupula/hair cells), an orientation gizmo, a toggleable gravity arrow (world-fixed or head-fixed), an eyeball scene rendering torsional, vertical, and horizontal nystagmus as three composed rotations, and a VNG-style trace plotting all three components over time.

2Physics models

Six coupled mechanisms carry the simulation from head orientation to eye movement. Each is a small, deliberately simplified model rather than a full fluid-dynamics simulation — chosen so its qualitative behavior (latency, fatigue, direction) matches the clinical picture.

Canal geometry canal.ts

Each (canal, side) pair gets a cached orthonormal in-plane basis (e1, e2) built from a literature plane normal. A point on the duct is R·cos(s)·e1 + R·sin(s)·e2, with s = 0 at the ampulla and increasing s ampullofugal. For the posterior canal, e1 is anchored to gravity's in-plane projection when upright — making the ampulla the natural resting point. The horizontal canal cannot use that same trick (it would tautologically force the ampulla to be the low point regardless of real anatomy), so its e1 is instead anchored to the real ampulla direction taken from cadaveric centerline data, and gravity's true resting arc position (~106° from the ampulla) falls out as a computed quantity.

CANAL_RADIUS_M0.0032 mduct radius, literature approx.
S_MAX2π×0.9radians of duct modeled
S_COMMON_CRUS3.5 rad"cleared the duct" threshold

Canalithiasis clot transport canalith.ts

An overdamped Stokes-drag model: instantaneous target velocity is proportional to gravity's tangential component at the clot's current position. Two temporal gates reproduce the clinical latency-then-paroxysm pattern rather than an instant response: a breakaway latency requiring a consistent driving direction sustained for LATENCY_SECONDS before any motion starts, and a first-order inertia lag toward the target velocity, giving a visible ramp-up and ramp-down rather than a snap.

LATENCY_SECONDS2.5 sbreakaway delay
CLOT_INERTIA_TAU0.8 svelocity ramp time constant

Once the clot reaches the far wall (s = S_MAX) it is marked clearedIntoUtricle and frozen — a real otoconial mass swept into the utricle is mechanically lost to the duct's fluid dynamics, so an ordinary subsequent head tilt cannot "un-clear" it. That freeze is deliberately keyed to full settlement rather than the earlier S_COMMON_CRUS threshold, so a maneuver still mid-sweep through the crus-to-utricle stretch stays reversible.

Cupula relaxation cupula.ts

A semi-implicit first-order relaxation toward a drive-proportional deflection: βnew = (βold + κ·dsdt·dt) / (1 + dt/τ). Fatigue and decay patterns emerge from this single relaxation rather than being separately scripted.

KAPPA_FLOW1.0flow-to-deflection gain
TAU_CUPULA4.0 srelaxation time constant

Cupulolithiasis drive cupulolithiasis.ts

Debris fixed at s = 0 feeds the same relaxation with a drive proportional to gravity's tangential component at that fixed point — no position to integrate, no breakaway latency, no clot inertia. This reproduces the distinguishing clinical signature of cupulolithiasis: minimal-latency, non-fatiguing nystagmus that persists for as long as the provoking position is held, converging to a steady-state deflection βss = κ·τ·CUPULA_GRAVITY_GAIN·gcomponent.

CUPULA_GRAVITY_GAIN0.08calibrated so peak deflection matches canalithiasis's own paroxysm magnitude

Canal-specific mechanical release cupulaRelease.ts

A rapid head movement can mechanically knock cupula-adherent debris loose, converting cupulolithiasis into free-floating canalithiasis mid-maneuver. Release is triggered when the smoothed angular acceleration about that specific canal's own plane-normal axis exceeds a threshold — projecting onto the canal's own axis (rather than raw omnidirectional angular speed) is what makes a posterior-plane maneuver like Semont release posterior debris without also releasing horizontal debris.

RELEASE_DECEL_THRESHOLD13 rad/s²scripted maneuvers
INTERACTIVE_RELEASE_DECEL_THRESHOLD40 rad/s²mouse/gyro, ~3× higher
RELEASE_ACCEL_SMOOTHING_TAU0.1 slow-pass before differentiating

Short-arm re-entry shortArmReentry.ts · disabled

A distinct short passage directly between the utricle and the ampulla (alongside the main long-arm duct already modeled), through which settled debris can slide back in if a repositioning maneuver's final seated position isn't tilted nose-down enough. Modeled as two straight segments through an approximate waypoint, with the same latency-and-inertia structure as ordinary canalithiasis but independently time-scaled. This pathway exists in the code but is currently switched off in the running app (see §5).

VOR & Ewald's laws vor.ts

Slow-phase eye velocity is gain×β, with gain reduced for inhibitory-direction flow — Ewald's second law, that excitatory (ampullofugal for vertical canals, ampullopetal for the horizontal canal) responses are larger than inhibitory ones. A quick-phase reset fires once accumulated eye angle exceeds a threshold. The single combined eye rotation (about the canal's own tilted plane normal) is then linearly decomposed onto torsional, vertical, and horizontal axes via direction cosines — an approximation valid at the modest deviation angles the simulator operates at, not a full 3D Euler decomposition.

GAIN_VOR0.6excitatory-direction gain
INHIBITORY_GAIN_FRACTION0.5Ewald's-second-law asymmetry
QUICK_PHASE_THRESHOLD0.35 radreset trigger

3Assumptions & simplifications

Every item below is flagged in the source itself as a deliberate approximation, not silently smoothed over.

  • Geotropic/apogeotropic sign as a single flip. debrisOnUtricularSide stands in for a full attachment-geometry model and is explicitly not independently verified against real clinical VOG recordings.
  • Clot inertia as a visualization device. CLOT_INERTIA_TAU is documented as standing in for the clinically-described acceleration/deceleration phases, not a literal second-order mass-and-drag derivation.
  • Teaching-time compression. The mobility constant driving clot speed is a physically-derived value multiplied by a TEACHING_MODE_TIME_SCALE of 19× purely for legibility — a real-time mode is not implemented, though the underlying physical constant is retained in code for future use. The same compression is applied independently to the short-arm pathway and to every scripted maneuver's hold durations (Zuma's real ~3-minute holds compress to roughly 20 seconds on screen).
  • Linear eye-decomposition. Torsional/vertical/horizontal components come from projecting one combined rotation onto three fixed direction cosines, valid only at the modest deflection angles this simulator produces.
  • Short-arm geometry as two straight segments through an approximate waypoint, rather than a traced centerline — a smooth spline was explicitly judged as "false precision" given the underlying data's own resolution.
  • Horizontal-canal tilt magnitude comes out ~16° from true horizontal in this model, versus a commonly quoted ~30° in clinical teaching — left as a flagged, unresolved discrepancy rather than force-fit (see §5).
  • Maneuver waypoint angles are teaching approximations, not patient-specific or clinically validated positioning angles.
  • Cupula elevation direction in the 3D scene is schematic. An earlier attempt to derive it from real per-canal cupula landmarks was tried and rejected: those landmarks turned out to be mostly in-plane with the canal (dot product only ~0.1–0.15 with the plane normal), which would have rendered debris 5–7 mm off target.
  • Mesh alignment by translation only, not rotation — per-canal rotation of the whole real-anatomy assembly was tried and rejected in favor of plain translation.
  • The Zuma maneuver is a pure kinematic waypoint sequence, not coupled to a forced pathology or debris-side selection — a known, named simplification.
  • Anterior canal is entirely unmodeled in the physics layer, despite its anatomy existing in the underlying dataset.

5Unresolved issues

Flagged directly in the source as open, disabled, or unverified.

Disabled feature Short-arm re-entry is implemented but switched off in the running app (SHORT_ARM_REENTRY_ENABLED = false) — it was firing too easily in practice and is disabled pending further tuning, without deleting the pathway itself.
Unresolved discrepancy The horizontal canal's derived tilt (~16° from true horizontal) is smaller than the ~30° commonly quoted in clinical teaching for "nose-down to bring the horizontal canal into true horizontal." Flagged as a magnitude question, not a sign question — the coplanarity cross-checks strongly support the direction being correct.
Unverified against clinical data The debrisOnUtricularSide geotropic/apogeotropic sign convention has not been independently checked against real VOG recordings.
Estimated, not measured The interactive release threshold's ~3× scaling relative to the scripted-maneuver threshold has no independent interactive-specific acceleration data behind it — it mirrors the ratio used for the predecessor speed-based threshold.
Known alignment tolerance The real-anatomy overlay's axes sit ~7.7° (posterior) and ~8.5° (horizontal) off the literature plane-normal directions, and centerline-to-mesh position agreement holds to within roughly 0.2–0.3 mm, not exactly.
Not yet built No uncompressed, real-time physics mode exists; the underlying physical mobility constant is exported and ready, but nothing in the UI currently exposes it.

6Proposed improvements

Ranked roughly by how directly each closes a gap already named in §5, from clinical-accuracy work to teaching-experience polish.

Re-tune and re-enable short-arm re-entryclinical accuracy

The pathway is built and citation-backed but disabled because it fires too easily. Re-deriving its mobility scale from the same empirical settling speed used elsewhere, rather than an independently chosen teaching multiplier, would let it ship without a second ad hoc constant.

Validate the geotropic/apogeotropic sign against real VOG tracesclinical accuracy

A single sign flip currently stands in for the full attachment-geometry distinction. Comparing simulated horizontal-canal nystagmus direction against published geotropic vs. apogeotropic VOG recordings would either confirm the shortcut or reveal where it breaks down.

Resolve the horizontal-canal tilt discrepancyanatomical fidelity

Reconciling the derived ~16° figure against the clinically quoted ~30° — likely by cross-checking against a second independent anatomical dataset — would settle a magnitude question the codebase currently just documents rather than resolves.

Full 3D eye-rotation decompositionphysics depth

Replacing the linear direction-cosine projection with a proper Euler/quaternion decomposition would remove the small-angle caveat and let the eye model handle larger provoked deflections accurately, e.g. for exaggerated teaching demonstrations.

Expose an uncompressed, real-time physics modefeature

The physically-derived mobility constant already exists in code; surfacing a toggle between "teaching speed" and "real time" would let advanced users see genuine clinical timing (multi-minute Zuma holds and all) without touching the underlying model.

Model the anterior canalcoverage

Anatomical data for the anterior canal already exists in the underlying dataset; wiring it through the same canal-basis/canalith/cupula pipeline would complete coverage of all three canal pairs, at the cost of a rarer clinical presentation.

Independent interactive-release calibrationphysics depth

Capturing real mouse-drag/gyro acceleration traces from users and measuring where accidental release actually starts would replace the current borrowed 3× scaling factor with a measured one.

Scenario presets and a guided teaching modeteaching experience

A short set of named starting configurations (e.g. "apogeotropic horizontal cupulolithiasis, right ear") with a guided walkthrough of the matching maneuver would lower the barrier for students encountering the simulator without prior canal-selector familiarity.

7Closing assessment

The simulator's defining trait is not any single physical model — each mechanism (Stokes-drag clot transport, first-order cupula relaxation, linear VOR gain) is intentionally simple — but the discipline with which every simplification, citation, and rejected alternative is recorded next to the code it governs. That makes the open questions in §5 tractable rather than hidden: each is a specific, scoped, already-partially-investigated gap rather than an undocumented guess. The improvements in §6 are, in that sense, mostly a matter of finishing work the codebase has already started and flagged for itself.
